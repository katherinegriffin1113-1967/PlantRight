## **D — Smart Market Questions**

### **What keeps them awake at night, eyes open, staring at the ceiling**

She is running a mental inventory of everything she wants to improve about the house and the yard is always on the list. She knows the front of her house could look so much better. She has seen her neighbor's yard and it bothers her more than she will admit out loud. She is also quietly calculating how much money she has already thrown at plants that did not make it and wondering if she just does not have a green thumb or if she is doing something fundamentally wrong.

### **What they are secretly afraid of in life**

That she is just not the kind of person who can have a nice yard. That it is somehow a talent she was not born with. She will never say this out loud because she is a competent, capable adult, but somewhere underneath the Pinterest boards and the optimism is a fear that beautiful outdoor spaces are for other people who know something she does not.

### **What they are angry about and who they are angry at**

She is not dramatically angry, but she is genuinely frustrated with the garden center industry. The tags on plants are useless. 'Full sun' means nothing when she does not know how many hours of direct sun her south-facing side bed actually gets. She is frustrated that nursery employees either do not know the answer or give her a confident answer that turns out to be wrong.

### **Top 3 Daily Frustrations**

- The gap between what her yard looks like and what she knows it could look like

- Feeling like she does her research but still ends up with the wrong plant for the wrong spot

- Spending money on something she cannot return when it fails, with no explanation for what went wrong

### **Biggest Secret Desire in Life**

To have a yard that makes people slow down when they drive by. Not showy. Just genuinely beautiful, well-considered, and clearly loved.

### **Built-In Bias to How They Make Decisions**

She trusts systems and specificity over general advice. If someone tells her 'plant lavender, it's easy,' she is skeptical. If something tells her 'based on your address, your front left bed receives 4.2 hours of direct sun and has clay-heavy soil, and here are three plants with a 94% survival rate in those conditions,' she is sold.

## **F — Purchasing Habits**

### **Top 3 Decision Triggers**

- Specificity: she buys when the solution feels tailored to her situation, not generic

- Social proof from people like her — not influencers with a perfect studio setup and a sponsored garden

- Low risk entry: a free trial, low price point, or money-back framing removes her hesitation fast

### **Prior Purchases For This Pain**

- Multiple rounds of plants from Home Depot, Lowe's, and local nurseries totaling $300-$600 over two to three seasons

- One or two gardening books she skimmed and set aside

- A soil test kit she bought, used once, and was not sure how to act on the results

- A one-time consult with a landscaper she felt overcharged her for basic advice

### **Price Tolerance**

Comfortably $9.99 to $29.99 for a one-time purchase or annual subscription. She has spent more than that on plants that died in a month. If the value is framed correctly, she does not need to be convinced on price — only on whether it will actually work for her specific yard.

### **Time Horizon of Solution**

She wants to see results this season. She is not planting a legacy garden. She wants to put something in the ground this spring, watch it grow, and feel like she finally got it right.
